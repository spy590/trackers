NAM Training 101 · interactive

How a neural net learns to be your amp

A walk through the NAM A2 capture → train → model flow, with the core machine-learning idea behind each step. Built for a strong software engineer who's new to ML.

If you already know this, you know most of ML: training is just iterative optimization of a big pile of numbers against an error metric — a loop that nudges serialized state toward a goal, over and over, keeping the best version it sees. That's the whole game. Everything below is that idea, made concrete with your own Friedman capture.
STAGE 01

The data: a matched pair

You played a fixed dry reference signal (NAM's standardized sweep) into your amp and recorded the wet output. That pair — input.wav → output.wav — is the entire training set. The model's job: given the dry sample, predict the wet sample your amp would produce.

There's no giant labeled dataset here. One ~3-minute reamp pair is the data. The sweep is designed to exercise the amp across dynamics and frequencies so a few minutes is enough.

ML conceptSupervised learning · (X → Y) regression›
You hand the algorithm matched inputs (X = dry) and targets (Y = wet) and ask it to learn the function f(X) ≈ Y. Because the output is a continuous waveform (not a category), it's regression, not classification. Think of it as fitting a curve — just one with millions of knobs instead of slope + intercept.
STAGE 02

Alignment: line the pair up to the sample

The wet signal lags the dry by some samples (converters, the rig, cabling). Before training, NAM measures that delay from calibration blips in the sweep and shifts the pair so dry sample n matches wet sample n. On your capture the true delay was 22 samples (~0.5 ms).

This is the unglamorous step that decides everything. Our app first guessed the delay with a cross-correlation and got 101 — a 79-sample error. The model then trained on mismatched pairs and could never do well (ESR stuck ~0.43), no matter how long it trained. Garbage alignment in → an error floor no training can climb out of. (You'll feel this in Stage 7's toggle.)

ML conceptData preprocessing · label alignment›
Models are only as good as the (X, Y) pairing you feed them. A constant offset between input and label means you're teaching the net to predict output[n] from the wrong slice of input — a systematic labeling error. It caps achievable accuracy regardless of model size or training time. "Garbage in, garbage out," but quantitative.
STAGE 03

The model: a function with millions of knobs

The model is a WaveNet — a stack of 1-D convolutions. Concretely it's a function that takes a window of recent dry samples and outputs one wet sample. To predict sample n it looks back over a receptive field of ~6,000 samples (~132 ms) — enough context to capture how an amp's response depends on what just happened (sag, transients, decay).

The "knobs" are the network's weights — ~16k of them for the A2-Lite size, more for Full. Untrained, they're random and the output is noise. Training is the process of setting them.

ML conceptParameters / weights · the model is its numbers›
A neural net is a fixed architecture (the wiring — chosen up front) plus a big vector of weights (the learnable numbers). The architecture is your code; the weights are the config/state the optimizer fills in. A trained .nam file is literally that weight vector serialized — nothing more mysterious than a saved settings blob.
STAGE 04 · interactive

The training loop: nudge, measure, repeat

Now the core. We run epochs — full passes over the data. Each epoch is split into steps (batches). For every step: (1) push dry through the net to predict wet (forward pass), (2) measure how wrong it is with a loss — NAM uses ESR (error-to-signal ratio; lower = closer to your real amp), (3) backprop computes which way to nudge every weight, (4) the optimizer takes a small step in that direction. The same weights carry into the next epoch — each pass builds on the last. Watch the orange prediction crawl onto the green target as ESR falls:

ML conceptGradient descent · loss · backprop · learning rate›
The loss is a single number measuring wrongness (your objective to minimize). Backpropagation is the chain rule run backwards through the network to get the gradient — the direction in weight-space that reduces the loss fastest. Gradient descent steps the weights along it. The learning rate is the step size: too big overshoots/diverges, too small wastes time. It's just hill-descending on an error surface, one small step per batch. (An epoch = one full pass over the data; a step/batch = one chunk of it — like processing a dataset page-by-page.)
STAGE 05

Keep the best epoch, not the last

It's one continuous run, not many attempts — a common first assumption. But NAM holds out a slice of your capture it never trains on (a validation set), scores the model on it after every epoch, and snapshots whichever epoch scores best. On your 100-epoch A2-Full run the exported model was from epoch 94, not 100 — the last few epochs were no better, so it kept 94.

Why hold data out? A model can start memorizing quirks of the training audio (noise, a specific buzz) that don't generalize. The validation score catches that — if it stops improving while training loss keeps dropping, you're overfitting, and the best-validation checkpoint is your safe pick.

ML conceptTrain/validation split · overfitting · checkpointing›
Hold out data the optimizer can't see, and judge on that — it's your unit test against memorization. Overfitting = great on training data, worse on unseen data (fitting noise, not signal). Saving the best-validation epoch is model checkpointing / early-stopping: the final artifact is the best snapshot, not necessarily the final state. Like keeping your green CI build, not just the latest commit.
STAGE 06

One model, two sizes (A2 Full & Lite)

A2 trains a packed model: two sub-networks in one run — channels_8 (Full, pro audio) and channels_3 (Lite, low-CPU/embedded). One training pass, one .nam, two playable sizes. More channels = more capacity = lower error but more compute. On your capture, Full hit ESR 0.00568 and Lite 0.02598 — both good, Full noticeably tighter (exactly what its prediction-vs-target overlay showed).

ML conceptModel capacity · the accuracy ↔ size trade-off›
More parameters = more capacity to fit a complex function, at the cost of memory and per-sample compute. Picking a size is the classic engineering trade-off: a bigger binary that's more accurate vs. a small one that runs on a $3 chip. "Slimmable/packed" nets get both from a single train by sharing weights across widths — like shipping -O0 and -O3 from one build.
STAGE 07

The ESR floor: where does accuracy bottom out?

More epochs help — until they don't. Loss falls fast early, then flattens as the model approaches the best it can do for its size and data. Past that, extra epochs barely move it. Your runs make the point precisely:

Two different "floors" show up here. The healthy floor is capacity — A2-Full keeps improving past 100 epochs (0.00568 → 0.00288 at 500, ~2× better, and ~2× better than your A1-Standard 500-epoch reference): the floor for this recipe+data sits near ~0.003, below where 100 epochs lands, so the extra epochs genuinely buy accuracy here — just with steeply diminishing returns (the knee is ~epoch 50–150, then a long slow tail). The pathological floor is bad data — the misaligned run sits at ~0.43 forever (you saw it in Stage 4's toggle), and no number of epochs moves it. And note the recipe matters more than epochs: same 100-epoch budget, the packed A2-Full (0.006) beat our slimmable (0.078) by ~14×.

That 100→500 halving is why the app's default is now 500 epochs — the owner accepts ~1h45m per model (~21 min/100 epochs on the packed net) to bank the last ~2×; a per-job override drops it back down for quick tests.

ML conceptConvergence · diminishing returns · irreducible error›
Training converges — the loss curve is steep then asymptotic, like cache hit-rate vs. cache size. The asymptote is set by model capacity + data quality. Beyond it lies irreducible error: noise and mislabeling you can't train away. Throwing epochs at an irreducible floor is wasted compute — the fix is better data (alignment) or a better model (recipe/capacity), not more iterations.
STAGE 08

The artifact: a .nam is just the learned numbers

When training stops, NAM exports the best weights to a .nam file (JSON: the architecture descriptor + the weight vector + metadata like loudness and gear name). Loading it into a plugin or the A2 pedal is inference — running that same function forward, in real time, on your live guitar. No training, no gradients; just f(dry) → wet, sample by sample.

That's the round trip: capture a pair → align it → optimize a few million weights against an error metric → keep the best snapshot → serialize → play it back. The "AI amp" is a saved function.

ML conceptInference · a trained model is a deployable function›
Two phases, like build vs. run: training (expensive, offline, produces weights) and inference (cheap, real-time, consumes weights). Shipping the .nam is shipping a compiled artifact; the device just executes it. Everything you learned here happens once, at "build time"; your amp tone is the runtime.
